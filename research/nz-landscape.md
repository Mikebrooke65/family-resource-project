### Disproportionately Affected Groups
- Maori youth — depressive symptoms rose from 14% to 28% (2012-2019); suicide attempts doubled to 13%; suicide rate nearly twice non-Maori (16.3 vs 9.0 per 100,000 in 2022-23)
- Pacific youth (15-24) — suicide rates ~1.5x higher than Pacific adults (25-44)
- Rainbow/LGBTQ+ youth — 64% have thought about suicide; 10% have attempted it; over half report depressive symptoms
- Girls — depression rates consistently nearly double those of males
- High deprivation areas — suicide attempt rates double between deprivation tiers
- Disabled young people — nearly 1 in 2 report suicidal ideation
- Youth involved with Oranga Tamariki — almost 4x more likely to be affected

### The Trend
The deterioration has been rapid and consistent. Between 2012 and 2019, depression rates nearly doubled across all groups. Research (Sutcliffe et al. 2023, Fleming et al. 2024) confirms the decline is continuing. This is not a stable situation and no significant improvement has occurred.

## The NZ Research Base

NZ has unusually strong longitudinal data on youth mental health — this is an asset for building a credible resource.

### Youth2000 Survey Series (Key Source)
- Large-scale representative surveys of NZ secondary school students running since 2001
- Conducted by University of Auckland — Professor Terry Fleming is the key researcher
- Surveys: 2001, 2007, 2012, 2019 (Youth19)
- Measures: wellbeing, depression, self-harm, suicidality, substance use, sexual health
- Youth19 (2019): n=7,374-7,721 students aged 13-18
- Next survey (Youth24) data likely being analysed now — watch for publication
- Website: youth19.ac.nz

### Key NZ Researchers
- Professor Terry Fleming (University of Auckland) — leads Youth2000 series; also developed SPARX
- Professor Terryann Clark (University of Auckland) — Youth19 co-lead; focus on Maori and equity
- Kylie Sutcliffe — cluster analysis of Youth19 data; key papers on mental health profiles
- Dr Sarah Hetrick (University of Auckland) — self-harm and suicide prevention
- Professor Sarah Taufa / Dr F. Langridge — adapting online tools for safe self-harm communication (Cure Kids funded)

### Recent Key Studies
- Sutcliffe et al. (2023) — rapid and unequal decline in adolescent MH 2012-2019
- Sutcliffe et al. (2024) — cluster analysis identifying distinct profiles of need; 6.4% severe cluster
- Fleming et al. (2024) — increasing psychological distress; most won't meet CAMHS access criteria
- ScienceDirect (2025) — co-design study on barriers to self-harm management in NZ schools; identified lack of guidelines, lack of trust between schools and mental health services as key barriers

### Government Data
- Ministry of Health publishes quarterly youth self-harm hospitalisation data (biannual from 2024/25)
- Suicide Prevention Action Plan 2025-2029 — 34 actions; includes specific school self-harm resource actions (Action 1 and 2 under Ministry of Education)
- Budget 2025: $28M for health-led response to mental health 111 calls; $50M for mental health facility improvements

## The NZ System — How It Works (and Where It Fails)

### The Referral Pathway
1. Parent notices signs -> GP visit -> GP refers to CAMHS (if severe enough)
2. Schools can also refer via guidance counsellor or resource teacher
3. CAMHS assesses -> accepts (moderate-severe) or declines (mild-moderate)
4. If declined: signposted to community services — but these are fragmented

### The CAMHS Problem
CAMHS (Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services) is the specialist public service for young people with serious mental health needs.

- Only accepts moderate-to-severe cases — most self-harm presentations are declined
- Chronically under-resourced: Wellington CAMHS reported operating at half capacity due to staff leaving; requests for additional staff turned down
- Wait times: families report 3-6+ months from referral to first appointment — not therapy, just assessment
- A 2025 research paper found families left "frightened and frustrated" even when services were accessed
- Fleming et al. (2024): most young people with significant distress won't meet CAMHS access criteria — they fall into a gap

### What This Means for Parents
A parent whose child self-harms but doesn't meet the severe threshold for CAMHS is left with:
- A GP who may not know what to say
- A school counsellor who is overloaded and under-trained
- Fragmented community services that are hard to navigate
- No single trusted resource to turn to

This is precisely the gap we are filling.

### Online / Digital Tools — Full Audit (May 2026)

---

#### SPARX (sparx.org.nz)
- **What it is:** Online CBT-based e-therapy game for 12-19 year olds with mild-moderate depression and anxiety
- **Who built it:** University of Auckland (led by Professor Sally Merry and Professor Terry Fleming); built with gaming experts
- **Launched:** Trialled 2012 (published in British Medical Journal); nationally available free since April 2014 via Ministry of Health funding; now funded by Health New Zealand
- **Status:** Active and current — referenced in government helpline documents as recently as November 2025
- **Usage data (2025 study — Fleming et al., JMIR):** 21,320 adolescents registered over first 7 years (approx 2% of NZ 12-19 year olds); 63.6% started (13,564); mean age 14.9 years; 62.7% female
- **Completion:** Low — only 51% completed Module 1; 7.4% completed 4+ modules; 3.1% completed all 7. Key barriers were slow download speeds and device compatibility (originally desktop-only)
- **Effectiveness:** Where young people did engage, effect sizes were comparable to trial results — mean depression score reduced from 14.0 to 10.5 (effect size 0.51 for full completion)
- **Gaps/limitations:** Not self-harm specific. Completion rates are low in real-world use. Originally desktop-only (now mobile). Less effective for transgender youth. No parent-facing component.
- **Relevance to us:** Youth-facing, not parent-facing — complementary not competitive. Professor Terry Fleming (key researcher) is our top clinical advisor target. Strong referral relationship possible.

---

#### The Lowdown (thelowdown.co.nz)
- **What it is:** Youth-facing website for depression and anxiety information, stories, and support
- **Who built it:** Ministry of Health's National Depression Initiative (NDI)
- **Launched:** 3 December 2007 — one of NZ's oldest digital mental health tools
- **Status:** Still active and still referenced in official government resources (November 2025). However the original site was built in Adobe Flash (now obsolete) — the current version has been updated but still feels dated in design and tone
- **What it offers:** Information pages, depression self-tests, videos, personal stories from rangatahi and NZ celebrities, webchat support, text line (text 5626)
- **Gaps/limitations:** General depression and anxiety only — no self-harm specific content. Youth-facing not parent-facing. Design and celebrity navigator model is from 2007 — feels dated for today's rangatahi. No AI or interactive tools.
- **Relevance to us:** Not competitive — different audience (youth vs parents) and different topic (depression vs self-harm). Worth referencing as a signpost for young people. The webchat model is worth noting.

---

#### Headstrong (headstrong.org.nz) — includes Aroha chatbot
- **What it is:** Free mental health app for NZ teenagers using structured chatbot interactions; offers CBT, mindfulness, and resilience tools
- **Who built it:** University of Auckland Faculty of Medical and Health Sciences, built with RUSH (NZ digital agency); co-designed with rangatahi. Key researchers: Sarah Hetrick, Tania Cargo, Karolina Stasiak
- **Launched:** Aroha chatbot launched April 7, 2020 (COVID-19 response); Headstrong app launched 2022; Kia Haumanu (bicultural/bilingual upgrade) launched April 2023
- **Status:** Active and current — funded by Te Whatu Ora (Health New Zealand); referenced in official MoH and MHF resources November 2025
- **What it offers:**
  - Foundations — wellbeing and resilience training using CBT
  - Stress Detox — 3-week stress and anxiety course
  - Aroha/Kia Haumanu — bicultural chatbot using Matauranga Maori and te reo Maori; CBT and positive psychology
  - Sessions are short (5 minutes), chat-based, guided by virtual avatars representing diversity of NZ youth
- **AI approach:** Uses predefined rules and scripts written by clinical experts — NOT generative AI. Limited use of GenAI only to make conversations flow more naturally. This is a key design decision — keeps it safe and clinically controlled.
- **Usage:** COVID-19 launch: 393 registrations and 238 users in first 2 weeks. Ongoing usage data not publicly available.
- **User feedback:** Positive — "One of the best apps I've used... I didn't feel embarrassed or shy"; praised for non-judgmental tone
- **Gaps/limitations:** Youth-facing not parent-facing. Focus is general wellbeing/resilience — not self-harm specific. No parent component.
- **Relevance to us:** HIGH. This is the only NZ digital tool with an AI/chatbot component. The design approach (predefined scripts, not generative AI, clinically authored) is directly relevant to how we should design our parent AI assistant. The team (Hetrick, Stasiak) are potential contacts/advisors. The Kia Haumanu bicultural approach is a model for how we handle te ao Maori in our content.

#### depression.org.nz
- **What it is:** General information website about depression and anxiety for New Zealanders; part of the National Depression Initiative
- **Who runs it:** National Depression Initiative (government-funded); linked to the Depression Helpline (0800 111 757)
- **Launched:** Established as part of NDI — exact launch date unclear but predates 2010
- **Status:** Active — referenced in 2025 government resources. Offers The Journal (free online self-help CBT programme)
- **What it offers:** Depression information, self-tests, The Journal (structured online CBT self-help), resources for seeking support
- **Gaps/limitations:** General depression and anxiety — not youth-specific, not self-harm specific, not parent-facing. Design feels dated. The Journal is a useful self-help tool but not promoted heavily.
- **Relevance to us:** Low direct relevance — different audience and topic. Good to reference in our resource as a signpost for parents who are also struggling with their own mood.

---

#### Aunty Dee (auntydee.co.nz)
- **What it is:** Free online problem-solving tool using structured CBT-based problem-solving therapy; designed particularly for Maori and Pasifika audiences
- **Who built it:** Le Va (a Pasifika health organisation)
- **Launched:** Exact date unclear — referenced in resources from at least 2014 onwards
- **Status:** Still active and referenced in official resources November 2025
- **What it offers:** Guided problem-solving tool — user works through a problem step by step with "Aunty Dee" as the guide; CBT-based; culturally adapted for Pasifika audiences; clear crisis links
- **Clinical review:** Healthify rates it highly — "a brilliant website that provides a fun and interactive version of a traditional cognitive behavioural therapy tool"; clinicians report clients find it "simple and rewarding"
- **Gaps/limitations:** General problem-solving — not self-harm specific, not parent-facing, not youth-specific. Design is dated. No ongoing interactive/AI component.
- **Relevance to us:** Low direct relevance. The cultural adaptation model (Pasifika-specific framing) is worth noting — we should think about how we address Maori and Pasifika parents specifically given the disproportionate impact on those communities.

## Why Parents Don't Talk About It

1. Shame and guilt — parents feel it reflects personal failure; everyone suffers in silence believing they are alone
2. Misunderstanding — most associate self-harm with suicide attempts or attention-seeking (reality: usually emotion regulation)
3. School caution — contagion concerns mean schools rarely discuss it, leaving parents completely unprepared. A 2025 NZ research study found this is one of the key barriers to school-based self-harm management.
4. Clinical gaps — GPs and teachers often don't know what to say; CAMHS turns away most presentations
5. Extra taboo layer — even as general MH conversations improve, self-harm carries additional stigma
6. System confusion — the referral pathway is opaque; parents don't know where to start

This silence is both the problem and the opportunity.
