# Parent Emotional Journey and Search Behaviour Research

Last updated: May 2026  
Status: Complete — based on peer-reviewed research and keyword analysis  
Purpose: Essential reference for brand, content, tone of voice, design, and collateral

---

## Why This Document Exists

Before we name, design, or write a single word of content, we need to understand exactly what is happening for a parent in the moment they find us. This document captures what the research tells us about the parent emotional experience — from the moment of discovery through to the long term — and what they are actually searching for and why.

This is the foundation for every design and content decision we make.

---

## Part 1: The Parent Emotional Journey

Based on multiple peer-reviewed studies including:
- Martin et al. (2024) systematic review — Child and Adolescent Mental Health
- Raphael et al. qualitative study — British Journal of Psychiatry
- MDPI study (2020) — Parents' Experience and Psychoeducation Needs
- Emerging Minds (2025) — Understanding parents' experiences
- Springer Nature qualitative study (37 parents, in-depth interviews)

---

### Stage 1 — The Moment of Discovery

**How it happens:**
- Finding cuts, burns, or scars
- A teacher or school counsellor calls
- A friend of the child tells them
- They find something online or on a device

**Immediate emotions (documented across all studies):**
- Shock — the universal first response
- Horror and disbelief
- Fear — "are they going to die? Is this suicide?"
- Panic — "what do I do RIGHT NOW?"
- Physical symptoms — insomnia, nausea, loss of appetite, fatigue

**The first question in their head:**
> "Is this serious? Are they trying to kill themselves?"

**Design implication:** The very first thing our resource must do is answer this question — calmly, clearly, without alarm. Parents conflate self-harm with suicide. Correcting this misunderstanding is the most urgent content task.

---

### Stage 2 — The Hours and Days After

**Emotions:**
- **Guilt and self-blame** — the most consistently documented emotion across all research. "I failed as a parent." "How did I not see this?" "What did I do wrong?"
- **Shame** — "What will people think of us?" Parents withdraw from social contact; social isolation is documented
- **Confusion** — "I don't understand why they would do this"
- **Helplessness** — "I don't know what to say. I'm terrified of making it worse."
- **Walking on eggshells** — exact phrase used by parents in multiple studies; afraid to trigger another episode
- **Anger** — less openly expressed but present; sometimes misdirected at child
- **Hypervigilance** — constantly watching for signs; exhausting

**The questions in their head:**
- "Why is this happening?"
- "What do I say to them? What if I make it worse?"
- "Who do I call? Where do I go?"
- "Should I tell the school? Tell my family? Tell anyone?"
- "Am I a bad parent?"
- "Is this my fault?"

**Design implication:** Content must validate these feelings before providing information. "You are not a bad parent" is not just a nice thing to say — it is the most important thing to say, and it must come early.

---

### Stage 3 — Seeking Help

**What happens:**
Parents try to access support — GP, school counsellor, CAMHS. In NZ, most are turned away or face months of waiting.

**Emotions:**
- **Reluctance to seek help** — shame and guilt actively prevent outreach; fear of judgment
- **Frustration** — turned away by CAMHS; GP doesn't know what to say; school counsellor is overloaded
- **Isolation** — "I'm the only parent going through this" (they are not — 25-50% of NZ secondary students self-harm per year)
- **Second-guessing** — "Am I overreacting? Is this just a phase?"
- **Feeling not listened to** — documented emotion in research

**The need at this stage:**
Validation that they are doing the right thing by seeking help. Clarity on what the NZ pathway actually looks like. Someone who says: *you are not alone, and here is what to do next.*

**Design implication:** We need a clear, honest explanation of the NZ system — including what CAMHS is, why they may be turned away, and what to do next. This is content no other NZ resource provides.

---

### Stage 4 — The Long Haul

**What happens:**
Self-harm often continues for months or years. Parents sustain a prolonged period of stress that takes a significant toll.

**Emotions:**
- Chronic anxiety and hypervigilance
- Clinical depression — onset in parents documented in research
- Exhaustion — emotional and physical
- Grief — for the child they knew before; for the family life they expected
- Impatience when it doesn't stop — "it makes me cross"
- Loss of self — parents stop looking after themselves; neglect their own needs, relationships, other children

**The need at this stage:**
Peer support — other parents who understand. Self-care resources. Permission to look after themselves. Someone who says: *looking after yourself is not selfish, it is essential.*

**Design implication:** Phase 3 peer community and one-on-one coaching directly addresses this stage. Even in Phase 1, our content must include a section explicitly for parents on looking after themselves.

---

### The Six Core Psychoeducation Needs

A 2020 peer-reviewed study (MDPI) identified exactly six things parents need — this is the evidence-based content architecture for our site:

1. **Understanding the discovery** — making sense of what happened and why
2. **Understanding self-harm** — what it is, why young people do it, how it differs from suicide
3. **How to communicate** — what to say, how to approach the conversation without making it worse
4. **Navigating services** — how to get help in NZ, what to expect, what to do when turned away
5. **Looking after themselves** — parental self-care, their own mental health
6. **Psychoeducational resources** — practical tools and information they can use

**This is our content map. Every piece of content we create should sit within one of these six areas.**

---

### The Emotion Word Bank

From a 2024 systematic review (Martin et al.), these are the exact emotion words documented in the research literature — the language parents themselves use:

**Primary emotions:** guilt, shame, fear, shock, horror, panic, helplessness, confusion, disbelief

**Secondary emotions:** anger, frustration, impatience, sadness, grief, embarrassment, denial, numbness

**States:** walking on eggshells, at sea, lost, out of control, not listened to, isolated, alone, overwhelmed

**Physical:** insomnia, nausea, fatigue, loss of appetite

**Use these words in our content.** Not clinical language. Not "distress" and "emotional dysregulation." These words. This is how parents describe their own experience.

## Part 2: Search Behaviour Analysis

### The Search Moment

The parent is searching:
- Often late at night — after discovering something, after a call from school
- In a state of fear, shock, and shame
- Often in secret — they haven't told anyone yet
- Feeling like they are the only parent this has ever happened to
- Desperate for someone to tell them it will be OK

**They are not looking for clinical information first. They are looking for reassurance, understanding, and then practical next steps — in that order.**

---

### Search Patterns by Stage

**Immediate/crisis searches (first hours after discovery):**
- "my child is self harming what do I do"
- "found cuts on my daughter"
- "my teenager is cutting themselves"
- "is self harm the same as suicide"
- "self harm NZ help"
- "child self harm what to do"
- "found marks on my child's arms"

**Understanding searches (days after):**
- "why do teenagers self harm"
- "how to talk to child about self harm"
- "self harm not suicidal"
- "signs child is self harming"
- "what causes self harm in teenagers"
- "self harm emotion regulation"

**Help-seeking searches:**
- "self harm support NZ parents"
- "CAMHS self harm NZ"
- "help for parents of self harming teen NZ"
- "self harm counsellor NZ"
- "Yellow Brick Road self harm"

**Longer term/ongoing:**
- "my child keeps self harming"
- "self harm not getting better"
- "parent support self harm NZ"
- "how long does self harm last"
- "will my child stop self harming"

---

### What They Need When They Land on a Page

Research and search intent analysis tells us parents need three things, in this order:

**1. Immediate reassurance** — "Is my child going to be OK? Is this suicide?"
**2. Understanding** — "Why is this happening? What does this mean?"
**3. Practical next steps** — "What do I do right now?"

**Design implication:** Every landing page and piece of content must answer question 1 before anything else. Do not lead with statistics, clinical information, or navigation. Lead with: *you are not alone. Your child is not trying to die. Here is what this means.*

---

### The Click Decision

In a search results page, a frightened parent will click based on:
- Does this look like it's for me (not my child)?
- Does this feel safe and calm (not alarming)?
- Does this look trustworthy?
- Does the title/description speak to what I'm feeling right now?

**Implication for our name and meta descriptions:** The name and the first line of our Google description need to do enormous work. They must say "this is for you, you are not alone, you are in the right place" in under 10 words.

## Part 3: Implications for Brand and Design

### Tone of Voice Principles (derived from research)

1. **Parent to parent** — not clinical, not authoritative, not a health service. The voice of someone who has been through this and come out the other side.
2. **Calm, not alarming** — parents are already frightened. Every word must reduce anxiety, not increase it.
3. **Validating before informing** — always acknowledge feelings before providing information.
4. **Direct without being blunt** — say the hard things clearly but with warmth.
5. **Hopeful** — there is a way through this. This is the thing no other resource tells parents.
6. **Plain language** — no clinical terms. Use the words parents use.

### What the Name Needs to Do

In the search moment a parent needs to feel, within half a second of seeing the name:
- *This is for me*
- *I am not alone*
- *This is a safe place*

The name does NOT need to describe the problem (self-harm) — that is the job of the subtitle, meta description, and content. The name needs to provide emotional orientation.

### Colour, Design, and Feel

Based on the emotional state of the audience:
- **Warm not clinical** — no hospital whites or NHS blues
- **Calm not alarming** — no reds, no crisis aesthetics
- **Human not corporate** — photography of real people; handwritten elements; texture
- **Safe and trustworthy** — not slick or commercial; feels like a person, not a product
- **Accessible** — remember people are accessing this at challenging moments, possibly on a phone, possibly in distress; simplicity is essential

## Part 4: The Founding Story as Brand Asset

The research consistently shows that parents who feel understood are more likely to engage and stay. Our founding story — two parents who went through this with their own child and built the resource they wish had existed — is not just a nice backstory. It is a core brand asset.

The phrase "built by parents who have been there" should be prominent from day one. It is what distinguishes us from every clinical resource, every government website, every generic mental health page.

This is also why the tone must never drift toward clinical or institutional. The moment it does, we lose the thing that makes us different.
